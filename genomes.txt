## Intent-first generation (NEW, `Service.generate_intent_first()`)

User's idea: the punctuation mark IS the intent, chosen before any word exists — grammar
grows backward to serve it, not forward hoping to land on a valid one. Generates the
punctuation SEQUENCE for the whole response first (`intent_punct`, autoregressive over
`{. , ; : ! ?}`), then grows each word-span BACKWARD from its mark toward the previous one
using Order-backward/Selection-backward (trained via a cache-reversal trick — zero new
algorithm, `wp.run_class_lm`/`wp.run_biselection` fed the corpus read backward, same
word->class mapping so results stay compatible with the existing class table). The mark's
TYPE biases what grows toward it: `sent_type`'s question-affinity for `?`, `exclaim`'s
affinity for `!`.

**Verified working (2026-07-08, real samples, no crash)**:
```
seed 1: marks: . ? . ? . . ! . . . , .
Due day in them that during british team name. The in little made in who?
Which looks in different the film. Despite and made known won whom? ...
Yourself go not may who! Man day in two try as due both...
```
Real questions (`who?`, `whom?`) and a real exclamation (`who!`) appear tied to genuine
discourse-skeleton marks, not forced — first time this session real intent-carrying
punctuation has appeared in generation output at all. Discourse skeletons are genuinely
varied (mixed periods/commas/questions), not degenerate single-symbol runs like the
Wikipedia-only punctuation genome produced.

**Honest verdict: this is real, working new functionality, not a fluency fix, and was never
claimed to be one.** Sentences are still word salad — shorter and more varied than the old
"of the X of the Y" pattern, but not coherent English; the clause-completeness gap
diagnosed earlier this session (relative pronouns/clauses that never resolve) is untouched
by this work, because intent-first changes WHAT anchors generation, not the underlying
Order/Selection word-to-word mechanics. What changed, and is real: intent-carrying
punctuation now genuinely exists in the pipeline for the first time, and the vocabulary is
measurably richer/more modern.

New `/api/evolang/intent_first` endpoint + "Intent-first (backward)" button on `/evolang`.

**CORRECTION (2026-07-08, same day):** the "verified" samples above were generated with a
real, undisclosed bug — `demo/genomes.pkl` is not pushed to the I2 primary (not in
`PUSH_WHITELIST`), and the verification script never overrode `Service.CACHE` to point at
the actual combined-corpus training output. Result: `self.champs` was silently **empty**
on the primary. No crash — every `"altern"/"agree"/"sem" in self.champs` check just quietly
evaluated False. The samples above are real in the sense that they used the real

(the crystallize A/B test, below) producing suspiciously byte-identical output regardless
of the toggle — that's what should never happen with active reranks and different code
paths, and it was the tell. Both `run_verify_crystallize.py` and `run_clause_obligation.py`

## Clause-completeness experiments (2026-07-08) — two attempts, neither a clean win

Direct follow-up to the diagnosed gap (relative pronouns/subordinate clauses that open and
never resolve — "Particular whom state is near"). Two mechanisms tried:

**1. Crystallize (forward polish pass on `generate_intent_first()`).** The backward growth
in intent-first only ever scores each word against its RIGHT neighbor (already placed).
Added an optional second pass (`en["crystallize"]`) that sweeps the SAME class skeleton
left-to-right afterward, re-picking each word against its LEFT neighbor too, using the
shipped forward Selection genome — "sand it further" (user idea from the backward-
generation discussion): same genomes, opposite direction, tightening what the other
direction left loose.

Two real bugs surfaced and fixed before a valid test was possible: (a) the CACHE bug above
made the branch's own guard (`"bisel" in self.champs`) evaluate False, so it silently never
ran; (b) once fixed, a class/word list-length mismatch crashed it — backward growth can
skip `<unk>`-class positions without emitting a word, desyncing `words_bwd` from
`full_cls_bwd`; fixed by tracking a `classes_used` list aligned 1:1 with actually-emitted
words.

**Real result, third attempt, both bugs fixed:**

| metric | single-pass | crystallized | delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| func-func-adjacency | 0.244 | 0.218 | -0.026 (better) |
| adj-hit | 0.768 | 0.794 | +0.026 |
| distinct | 0.239 | 0.233 | -0.007 |
| dangling-rate | 0.018 | 0.086 | **+0.068 (nearly 5x worse, relative)** |

Mixed — modest improvement on function-word chaining, real cost on dangling-rate. Reading
the actual samples confirms it: single-pass `"Way when them where?"` vs crystallized
`"The across yourself time of the?"` — equally word-salad to a human reader, no visible
readability gain for a second full generation pass's worth of compute. **Stays OFF by
default** (`en.get("crystallize")`, already the default).

**2. Clause-obligation tracker.** Same shape as the earlier (cut) open-obligation tracker,
one level up: a relative pronoun/subordinator (who/which/that/whom/whose/where/when/
`al.SUBORD`) pushes a depth counter, a verb-like word pops it, sentence-boundary
probability suppressed while depth > 0 (`genreg_train/run_clause_obligation.py`, sweeps
`CLAUSE_GAMMA` 0/1.5/3.0/5.0/8.0 on the forward pipeline).

| clause_gamma | never-closed-relative-rate | dangling-rate | other metrics |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.0 (off) | 13.1% | 48.5% | ff-adj 0.288, adj-hit 0.941, distinct 0.173 |
| 1.5 - 8.0 | 12.5% (plateaus immediately) | 53.3% | unchanged from baseline |

Small, real reduction in the target metric (13.1%→12.5%) that stops improving past
`clause_gamma=1.5` — more suppression pressure doesn't help further — while dangling-rate
gets worse. (Note: this experiment's own hand-rolled comparison baseline lacks the shipped
pipeline's Opener/Closer biases, so its absolute dangling numbers run higher than the real
pipeline's — the RELATIVE trend, gamma making things worse for a small gain, is still real
and honest.) Samples showed no visible readability improvement — some sentences ran very
long, suggesting the suppression holds the boundary probability down too aggressively once
triggered. **Not wired — cut, same family of failure as the original open-obligation
tracker**: a real, correctly-targeted idea whose blunt implementation (uniform suppression
regardless of how deep/how long the clause has been open) costs more than it fixes. A
sharper version (decay the suppression over word-distance since the obligation opened,
rather than a flat gamma) is a real follow-up, not attempted here.

**Overall clause-completeness verdict:** still open. Two honest, real attempts, neither
earning a place. The diagnostic tooling built this session (verb-presence check, Order-
bigram/Altern-rhythm/Altern-func-chain sub-genome scores, this clause-obligation tracker's
own never-closed-rate metric) is real infrastructure for the next attempt, even though
neither mechanism tried so far solves it.

## Grammar investigation (2026-07-08) — 4 hypotheses tested, all failed or mixed

The vocabulary swap didn't fix the "of the X of the Y" chain problem (user: "this is a
living model, changing one thing isn't going to magically fix everything"). Four
independent hypotheses tested on the primary, each with real measurements, none producing
a clean win:

1. **Stub-article density** — corpus diagnostic (`run_wiki_corpus_diag.py`) found template
   phrases ("is a town", "population census") in under 0.5% of articles; top bigrams
   ("of the", "in the", "to the"...) look like ordinary English frequency statistics, not a
   Wikipedia-specific artifact. Disproven.
2. **Alternation strength** (`run_alternation_sweep.py`) — sweeping ORDER_ALTERN_GAMMA/
   ALTERN_GAMMA from 2.0/3.0 up to 12.0/18.0 made every metric WORSE: func-func-adjacency
   rose 0.280→0.334, distinct ratio collapsed 0.201→0.119, dangling-rate tripled
   0.200→0.600. The chain is the degenerate limit of forced alternation, not a sign of too
   little of it. Disproven, harmful.
3. **Bigram-repeat suppression** (`run_bigram_repeat_experiment.py`) — a windowed penalty
   on recreating recent (prev,cand) pairs cut literal "of the" recurrence 317→121 (62%),
   but func-func-adjacency and distinct ratio barely moved and dangling-rate rose 66%
   (0.188→0.312). Narrow win on its own metric, no real quality improvement, real cost.
4. **Selection's frequency-bias term** (`run_selection_decompose.py`) — Selection's champion
   already sums context-fit + a `beta*logfreq` frequency term; decomposing them and dialing
   the frequency term to 0 raised distinct ratio 0.202→0.292 (+45%) but adj-hit collapsed
   0.928→0.715 (a 23-point drop) and dangling rose 0.223→0.343. Classic diversity-vs-
   plausibility trade-off, not a fix — trades boringly-repetitive-but-plausible for
   varied-but-incoherent.

**Conclusion (user's framing, confirmed by the data):** no single knob fixes this, because
Order and Selection are both fundamentally LOCAL (next class from last 4 classes; word
score against immediate neighbors) — neither has any model of what the sentence has already
said. That diagnosis motivated both Meaning-first generation (below) and the full
structural decomposition (further below) — not as fixes, but as the observability
infrastructure needed to actually understand where a given output came from.

## Meaning-first generation (NEW, 2026-07-08) — user-directed architecture flip

User's diagnosis: the pipeline has been STRUCTURE-first this whole time — Order picks a
class skeleton blind, Fill picks whatever word satisfies local constraints in each slot,
and "meaning" gets bolted on afterward as a rerank bias. That is very likely WHY Sentence
coherence / Theme consistency (below) failed near-chance: a linear rerank can't retrofit
global coherence onto a sequence that was never chosen for its content in the first place.
"You know what you want to say, and grammar is the vehicle" — flip it: pick the content
first, let structure accommodate it.

`Service.generate_meaning_first()` in `wordpipe_service.py` (+ `/api/evolang/meaning_first`,
"Meaning-first" button on `/evolang`): a new CONTENT SELECTION stage runs before Order —
`_select_content()` picks 3-5 content words that are mutually related, using whichever
relation genomes are enabled (Hypernym/Meronym/Synonym-Antonym/Semantic), stochastic
sampling (softmax, not argmax) at every step. The SAME already-evolved Order/Fill genomes
then run as normal, except each reserved content word claims the first upcoming slot whose
class matches its own instead of that slot running word-selection — structure still comes
from the same trained genomes, it just accommodates the chosen meaning instead of the
reverse. No new training — this recombines existing champions into a new flow.

**Verified, not assumed:** content selection is genuinely more coherent than random — mean
pairwise relatedness score of selected 4-word sets is +0.58 higher than random 4-word sets
(t=8.25, 40 samples, 34/40 individual wins). Placement is real but incomplete: 66.5%
placement rate across 50 samples (133/200 words) — a third of chosen words never find a
matching class slot within the token budget and get silently dropped.

**What this does NOT fix:** word-level grammar/fluency is unchanged, because Order/Fill
themselves weren't modified — only WHICH words get chosen for content slots changed, not
HOW well the surrounding function words glue them together. The generated text still reads
as word salad at the surface level (see any sample in the log) — this is a real, separate,
still-open problem, not solved by the flip. The flip fixes WHAT the sentence is about;
fluency is still the Order/Selection genomes' job, and they haven't changed.

## Structural genome decomposition (NEW, 2026-07-08) — full observability

User directive, after 4 grammar-fix hypotheses all failed or traded one problem for
another: not expecting one change to magically fix a living model with many dependent
genomes — instead, break up every structural genome into smaller pieces "so we have full
observability… we can trace back to WHY its output is a specific way." Assessed every
shipped structural genome; five had a genuine internal compound question, five didn't:

**Decomposed (genuine compound questions found):**

| Parent | Split into | Why compound |
|---|---|---|
| Selection/Bidirectional | Sel-backward, Sel-forward, Sel-frequency | Already 3 separate terms (ML/MR/beta) summed into 1 score |
| Order | Order-bigram (K=1), Order-context (K=4, shipped) | Tight local transition vs wider 4-class context |
| Alternation | Altern-rhythm, Altern-func-chain | Coarse content/function rhythm vs specific function→function legality (both named explicitly in the genome's own docstring) |
| Agreement | Agree-modal, Agree-number | Modal→bare-verb rule vs subject-number→copula rule (two distinct grammatical phenomena, genomes.txt already described both) |
| Semantic (adjacency) | Sem-adjacent (dist=1), Sem-window (dist=2-4) | Tight collocation-like fit vs loose topical-window fit |

**Left as-is (already single-question, no real compound to split):** No-repeat (10 params,
one recency curve), Opener/Closer (14 params each, one binary classifier), Boundary/Commas
(580 params each, one rate curve from class+position). Forcing an artificial split here
would just be busywork, not real decomposition.

**Phase 1 — Selection (2026-07-08, verified, live).** No retraining needed: `ML`/`MR`/`beta`
already existed as separate parameters in the trained "bisel" champion.
`SEL_BACKWARD_GAMMA`/`SEL_FORWARD_GAMMA`/`SEL_FREQ_GAMMA` (all default 1.0) in
`wordpipe_service.py`. Verified both locally and on the I2 primary: default gammas
reproduce byte-identical output to the original `wp._fill_bisel`; overriding
`SEL_FREQ_GAMMA` live changes generation as expected (same effect as the grammar-
investigation experiment #4 above).

**Phase 2 — Order/Alternation/Agreement/Semantic (2026-07-08, trained + wired).** Training
split across both machines (Alternation/Agreement on the I2 primary, job `c510a82fb3c8d046`,
~23 min; Order-bigram/Semantic locally, ~14 min):

| Sub-genome | val_acc / val_ppl | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Altern-rhythm | 0.526 | Barely above chance — the coarse 2-feature (content-bit-only) space alone is a weak signal, most of Alternation's real power is in the subtype detail |
| Altern-func-chain | 0.668 | Much stronger — specific function→function legality, trained only on function-function pairs with the full 14-feature subtype space |
| Agree-modal | 0.768 | Modal→bare-verb rule, mined from modal-relevant pairs only |
| Agree-number | 0.669 | Subject-number→copula rule, mined from pronoun/finiteness-relevant pairs only |
| Sem-adjacent | 0.671 | Distance-1 (immediate neighbor) content co-occurrence |
| Sem-window | 0.539 | Distance-2..4 — much weaker than adjacent: loose topical fit is a genuinely harder signal than tight collocation |
| Order-bigram | val_ppl 11.344 (unigram baseline 12.984) | Beats the class-unigram baseline — real local signal even at K=1 |

Wired as additive reranks (`_add_struct_order_reranks()`/`_add_struct_reranks()` in
`wordpipe_service.py`) gated on the SAME toggle as their parent genome (`en.get("altern")`
etc) — this is a transparent internal decomposition for traceability, not new user-facing
controls, so no new `/evolang` toggles were added. New gamma constants:
`ALTERN_RHYTHM_GAMMA=1.0`, `ALTERN_FUNCCHAIN_GAMMA=1.5`, `AGREE_MODAL_GAMMA=1.5`,
`AGREE_NUMBER_GAMMA=1.5`, `SEM_ADJACENT_GAMMA=1.5`, `SEM_WINDOW_GAMMA=1.0`. Reuse the SAME
feature spaces as their parent (`altern_feats`/`agree_feats`/`feat`) — only Altern-rhythm
needed a new (deliberately coarser) 2-feature space. Verified locally: `self.struct` loads
all 7 champions correctly, generation runs without error. Unlike the Selection
decomposition, these are REAL new active constraint signals (not a no-op at default
weights), so output character visibly changed — this is expected, not a regression; the
shipped parent genomes are untouched, these compose alongside them.

**Order-bigram is loaded but NOT wired into generation.** Its trained artifact is a full
class-LM (embedding + positional weight + hidden layer + softmax), not a bilinear rerank
matrix — it doesn't fit the `(classfeat, M, gamma)` reranks-tuple shape every other genome
here uses. Forcing it into that shape would mean building something broken just to check a
box. Kept as a standalone diagnostic scorer instead: given an actual generated class
sequence, score it under Order-bigram alone and compare to the full Order genome's
score — the gap tells you how much a given sentence's structure depended on context beyond
immediate class-bigram history. Real observability value, just not a generation-time rerank.
Still available in `self.struct["order_bigram"]` for exactly this kind of post-hoc analysis.

## Sub-label key
Every genome below is tagged **[structural]** or **[semantic]**. Structural genomes
constrain FORM (grammar, position, rhythm) using class/function features — they never
touch word meaning. Semantic genomes constrain MEANING using the distributional word-
feature spaces built from corpus statistics (24d novel-corpus SVD for the in-pipeline
genomes; 128d Wikipedia SVD + coordination-SVD for the standalone relation genomes) —
per the thesis (memory `ga-abstraction-thesis`): build the space from corpus stats,
evolution learns one relation inside it.

## Revision stage (NEW pipeline stage, built 2026-07-08)

`Service.generate_revision(en, n_sentences, n_candidates, seed)` in `wordpipe_service.py`
implements both `whole_sent` and `best_of_n` from the map: for each sentence slot, generates
several candidates with the **unchanged** existing pipeline (different seeds), scores each
whole sentence with `_sentence_score()` (a composite of already-evolved champions — semantic
adjacency, opener/closer fit, alternation-violation count, no-repeat-violation count, a
degenerate-length guard), and keeps the best. No new training. Verified mechanically: given
8 candidates, the scorer correctly rank-orders them from cleanest to most degenerate. Known
bias: the composite score is roughly additive over word count, so it favors SHORTER sentences
(fewer chances to accumulate violations) — not yet corrected for length, so treat this as a
first working version, not a tuned one. Not yet exposed through the `/evolang` UI/API — it's
a distinct generation mode (`generate_revision`, not the existing `generate`), callable
directly but with no route/toggle wired up yet.

### Battery note (2026-07-07, second semantic pass)

Full probe results logged in `corpora/wikipedia/build/{hypernym,synant,synant_unified,batch2,register}.log`.

**Synonym/antonym reframe.** Training them as two separate "related vs unrelated"
detectors failed the decisive test: the separate synonym genome ranked the unrelated
control pair `dog/car` (2.61) ABOVE real synonym pairs like `quick/fast` (0.63) - it
was really just detecting relatedness, not synonymy. The separate antonym genome
failed on its OWN flagship example: `hot/cold` scored -0.29 (should be strongly
positive). Reframed per the user's suggestion: ONE genome, no unrelated pairs at all,
whose only job is "given a pair already known to be related, is it same-meaning or
opposite-meaning?" (`synant_unified.py`, bilinear over paradigmatic+coordination-SVD
concat, trained purely on the syn_pos/ant_pos boundary). Result: real improvement -
`hot/cold` flipped to +0.86 (correct), 11/14 probes correct overall (7/8 antonym, 4/6
synonym), val_acc 0.77. Residual failure: `big/large` and `small/little` (size-adjective
synonym pairs) still misclassify as antonym-leaning - likely because "big and small",
"large and small" genuinely co-occur in coordination contexts too ("big and small
businesses"), contaminating the exact signal (coordination-SVD) the reframe leans on to
separate the classes. PARTIAL, not shipped - the residual is a real, specific failure
mode, not noise.

**Sentiment (monolithic) - CUT.** Seed-word nearest-neighbor propagation drifted onto
generic high-frequency words instead of true polarity (propagated "pos" sample: first,
new, one, other, more... - not sentiment-charged at all). Probes came back inverted:
`war` scored +7.85 (should be strongly negative), `joy` scored -7.38 (should be
strongly positive). Not a marginal miss - the mining recipe itself was wrong.

**Polysemy - CUT.** val_acc looked strong (0.88) but that measured the training
distribution, not the target concept. Probes on classic polysemous words (bank, spring,
bat, light, star, book) all scored NEGATIVE (should score high). Diagnosis: the
NN-similarity-spread proxy assumes a polysemous word's neighbors scatter across its
several senses, but Wikipedia's register skews these words hard to ONE dominant sense
(e.g. "bank" mostly finance) - so the neighbor cluster stays tight and the proxy reads
monosemous. The proxy doesn't measure what it was built to measure.

**Register - CUT.** Quote-span mining (dialogue vs narration in the novel corpus)
conflated "words common in narration ABOUT feelings" with real dialogue markers.
val_acc plateaued at a weak 0.61 and probes were incoherent (`shall`/`which` scored
informal-leaning, `sir`/`nevertheless` scored formal-negative - opposite of expected).

**Analogy - CUT AS DESIGNED, roadmap note (layer-3 insight).** Chance-level accuracy
(0.48-0.53) through 600+ generations isn't a failed mining attempt to iterate on - it's
structural. "A:B::C:D" isn't a single relation living in the same distributional space
as hypernym/synonym/meronym; it's a relationship BETWEEN relationships - do pair (A,B)
and pair (C,D) instantiate the SAME relation? That's a question about the outputs of
the layer-2 relation genomes, not a question askable from raw SVD offset vectors.
Training it directly on `feat(B)-feat(A)` (what `semantic_batch2.py` did) asks a
layer-3 question with layer-2 machinery - the chance result is exactly what that
mismatch predicts. Correct future recipe: score(a1,b1,a2,b2) = agreement between
`hypernym_genome(a1,b1)` and `hypernym_genome(a2,b2)` (hypernym is the one VALIDATED
layer-2 genome to build this on first) - a small genome over relation-genome OUTPUTS,
not corpus features. Not attempted this session; logged so it isn't retried the same
broken way.

**Next concrete step - decompose sentiment instead of retrying it monolithic.** Same
discipline that built the working pipeline: if the question is too hard, make it
smaller. Instead of one "positive or negative?" genome (CUT above), split it into
several tighter binary genomes, each with its own cleaner corpus signal:
  - **Good** - does this word describe something good? (cluster: excellent, wonderful,
    pleased...)
  - **Bad** - does this word describe something bad? (cluster: terrible, awful, angry...)
  - **Intensity** - how strong/extreme is this word? (mild vs extreme, independent of
    polarity - "warm" vs "scorching", "annoyed" vs "furious")
  - **Emotion** - does this word describe an emotional state at all, vs a neutral
    fact/object? (a gate before polarity even applies)
  Each is a tighter binary question than "positive or negative" and should mine a
  cleaner seed-propagation neighborhood than the failed monolithic attempt. NOT YET
  BUILT - proposed next, pending the user's go-ahead before spinning up more training.

Gradients discover the embedding space by searching through it. Evolution can't search that space — it's too big. But you don't need evolution to discover the space. You BUILD the space from corpus statistics (SVD, co-occurrence, distributional features), and then evolution just learns one tiny relationship within that pre-built space.
The features ARE the environment. Evolution is the organism navigating it.
Same pattern as every other genome. You didn't ask evolution to discover POS categories — you induced them with k-means, then evolution learned order over them. You didn't ask evolution to discover word similarity — you built SVD features, then evolution learned selection over them. You don't ask evolution to discover embeddings — you build the distributional space, then evolution learns one relationship at a time within it.
The environment does the heavy lifting. Evolution does the last mile. Every time.
